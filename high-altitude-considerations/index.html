Staying Healthy

Stay safe and comfortable in the mountains

Health at High Altitude

We love the mountains (and hope you do too!), but there are a few health and safety factors to keep in mind when planning a trip at altitude.

Weather

Weather in the mountains is variable and can change quickly.  During the summer months, it's common to have sunny days, afternoon thunderstorms, and chilly evenings, so please pack accordingly--don't forget your sunscreen, raincoat, and a warm jacket.  We expect highs to be in the 80s with lows in the evenings in the 50s.

Altitude Sickness

Spending time at high elevation can cause altitude sickness in some people.  This is usually not dangerous, but is uncomfortable, so please take precautions so you can enjoy the party!  Anyone can be affected, but people with heart or breathing trouble are most likely to experience problems.  Please read more about prevention and signs and symptoms below.

Water & Alcohol

Mountain air is dry air, and you'll need to drink lots of water to stay hydrated and healthy.  Many people also find lip balm and lotion helpful for staying comfortable.

The effects of alcoholic drinks are also stronger than they would at sea level.  A good rule of thumb is that a drink will affect you about three times as much as normal, so please drink responsibly.

Preventing Altitude Sickness

A few tips

  • If you have time, it helps to spend a night at lower altitude (like Durango) before heading higher (like Silverton and Eureka).
  • Take it easy; don’t over-exert yourself when you first get up to altitude. Light activity during the day can help the acclimatization process.
  • The acclimatization process is inhibited by dehydration, over-exertion, and alcohol and other depressant drugs.
  • Stay properly hydrated. Acclimatization is often accompanied by fluid loss, so you need to drink lots of fluids to remain properly hydrated (at least 3-4 quarts per day). 
  • Avoid tobacco and alcohol and other depressant drugs including, barbiturates, tranquilizers, and sleeping pills. These depressants further decrease the respiratory drive during sleep resulting in a worsening of the symptoms at night.
  • Eat a high carbohydrate diet (more than 70% of your calories from carbohydrates) while at altitude.
  • If you have any questions or concerns about how altitude could affect you, consider talking to your doctor.
  • Doctors can also prescribe medication to help you acclimatize more quickly.
